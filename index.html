Agricultural Waste into Bioenergy Resource

27/06/2026

Malaysia's agricultural sector generates a vast amount of biomass annually, transforming waste into a pathway for a circular economy[cite: 3, 5]. Click to read the full report.

Overview & Statistics

Malaysia's agricultural sector generates a vast amount of biomass annually, which is mainly from rice, rubber, palm oil and coconut production[cite: 3]. The large amount of biomass is usually disposed of in an environmentally harmful way such as open burning, improper landfilling or incineration[cite: 4]. The agricultural waste-to-energy method offers a pathway to a circular economy[cite: 5].

  • In 2022, Total Biomass reached 182.6 million tonnes[cite: 43].
  • Every year, Malaysia generates approximately 168 million tonnes of biomass, with the palm oil industry contributing the vast majority (roughly 94%)[cite: 59].

Waste-to-Energy Processes

Agricultural biomass is converted into energy through four main technological pathways[cite: 46]:

  • Direct Combustion (Incineration): The most common method where biomass is burned in a boiler to produce high-pressure steam, which drives a turbine to generate electricity[cite: 47].
  • Anaerobic Digestion: A biochemical process where microorganisms break down organic waste in the absence of oxygen to produce biogas (methane and CO2), which can be used for heat or power[cite: 48].
  • Gasification: A thermochemical process that uses partial oxidation at high temperatures (typically 800°C–1400°C) to convert biomass into "syngas" (a mixture of hydrogen and carbon monoxide). [cite: 49].
  • Pyrolysis: The heating of biomass in the total absence of oxygen, producing bio-oil, biochar, and syngas. It is highly efficient for creating liquid fuels.
  • Torrefaction & Pelletization:Physical and mild heat treatments (200°C–300°C) that increase the energy density and grindability of biomass, turning it into wood pellets or briquettes for easier transport and more efficient burning.

Hydrogen-Ready Power Plant (Singapore)

27/06/2026

A new 600MW hydrogen-ready combined cycle power plant is being developed for Sembcorp in Singapore, operational by 2026[cite: 74]. Click to read the full report.

Project Overview

On 24th May 2023, a consortium comprising Mitsubishi Power and Jurong Engineering Limited (JEL) has been awarded an Engineering, Procurement and Construction (EPC) contract to develop a new 600MW hydrogen-ready combined cycle power plant (CCPP) for Sembcorp Industries' fully owned subsidiary Sembcorp Cogen Pte Ltd[cite: 74]. The plant is expected to be operational by 2026[cite: 74].

How It Works

It utilizes the Mitsubishi Power M701JAC gas turbine, which is an advanced Combined Cycle Gas Turbine (CCGT)[cite: 76]. Unlike standard power plants that only produce electricity, this cogeneration plant simultaneously produces steam[cite: 77]. This steam is piped directly to industrial customers on Jurong Island for manufacturing processes, maximizing the total thermal efficiency of the fuel[cite: 78]. By using this "Combined Cycle" (burning gas + using the heat for steam), the plant achieves an efficiency of over 60%, which is significantly higher than traditional power plants[cite: 87].

Hydrogen Phasing & Blending

The plant doesn't run on 100% hydrogen on Day 1[cite: 79].

  • Phase 1 (Immediate): The plant runs primarily on Natural Gas but is technically capable of burning a blend of 30% Hydrogen by volume with zero hardware modifications[cite: 80].
  • Phase 2 (Future): The turbine is designed with a "provision" to be retrofitted to burn 100% Hydrogen once the regional supply chain (ammonia imports or local electrolysis) becomes commercially viable[cite: 81].
  • Fuel Blending: The turbine is equipped with Dry Low (DLN) combustors[cite: 83]. These are specially designed nozzles that mix hydrogen and natural gas in a way that prevents flame from traveling back into the pipe because hydrogen burns so fast[cite: 84].
